Introduction: Free trial screener

At the time of this experiment, Udacity courses currently have two options on the home page: "start free trial", and "access course materials". If the student clicks "start free trial", they will be asked to enter their credit card information, and then they will be enrolled in a free trial for the paid version of the course. After 14 days, they will automatically be charged unless they cancel first. If the student clicks "access course materials", they will be able to view the videos and take the quizzes for free, but they will not receive coaching support or a verified certificate, and they will not submit their final project for feedback.

In the experiment, Udacity tested a change where if the student clicked "start free trial", they were asked how much time they had available to devote to the course. If the student indicated 5 or more hours per week, they would be taken through the checkout process as usual. If they indicated fewer than 5 hours per week, a message would appear indicating that Udacity courses usually require a greater time commitment for successful completion, and suggesting that the student might like to access the course materials for free. At this point, the student would have the option to continue enrolling in the free trial, or access the course materials for free instead.

The hypothesis was that this might set clearer expectations for students upfront, thus reducing the number of frustrated students who left the free trial because they didn't have enough timewithout significantly reducing the number of students to continue past the free trial and eventually complete the course. If this hypothesis held true, Udacity could improve the overall student experience and improve coaches' capacity to support students who are likely to complete the course.

The unit of diversion is a cookie, although if the student enrolls in the free trial, they are tracked by user-id from that point forward. The same user-id cannot enroll in the free trial twice. For users that do not enroll, their user-id is not tracked in the experiment, even if they were signed in when they visited the course overview page.

 

Experiment Design

 

Metric Choice

The various metrics available to us are:

         Number of cookies

         Number of user-ids

         Number of clicks

         Click-through-probability

         Gross conversion

         Retention

         Net conversion

 

Number of cookies: Since the unit of diversion is a cookie, Number of cookies is expected to be comparable between the control and the experimental group. Hence this can be used as an invariant metric but not as an evaluation metric.

 

Number of user-ids: User-ids are generated once an individual enrols in a free trial, and the proposed change should affect the number of enrolments and therefore the number of user-ids. Hence this cannot be used as an invariant metric. User-id alone is a number. For tracking the propagation of users from one level to the next, a probability is a better choice. The metrics gross-convertion and retention, both take the number of User-ids into account while calculating the probability. So, it cannot be considered as an evaluation metric as well.

Number of clicks: Click on the start free trial button happens before the change is encountered. Hence, number of clicks is also not expected to vary across control and experiment groups and can be used as an invariant metric but not as an evaluation metric.

 

Click-through-probability: Click through probability is a combination of Number of cookies and Number of clicks. Since, number of cookies and number of clicks are invariant; Click-through-probability is also expected to be invariant and cannot be used as an evaluation metric.

 

Gross conversion: Gross conversion=(Number of user-ids to complete checkout and enroll in the free trial)/Number of cookies. As explained above, number of user-ids is expected to vary across control and experiment groups. Hence, gross conversion will also vary. So, it cannot be used as an invariant. If we try to think about the meaning of this metric, it means what percentage of users get enrolled with/without going through the screener. We want to measure the impact of introducing the screener on enrolment and hence gross-conversion should be used as an evaluation metric.

 

Retention: Retention rate will vary similar to gross conversion as the number of userids is expected to vary. Hence, it cannot be used as an invariant metric. However, this will help us to measure the impact of introducing the screener on the drop-out rate. So, it should be used as an evaluation metric.

 

Net conversion: This could help us measure whether the screener had any impact on the 14-day completion rate although it wont be able to tell us whether it was due to less students getting enrolled in the course. This is an important metric to track as it can provide an overall picture and can be used as an evaluation metric. We want to study whether the screener had an impact on the number of users who complete the 14 day trial. So, we cannot assume it to remain invariant across the control and experiment groups.

The whole objective of this experiment is, as mentioned in the introduction, to reduce the number of frustrated students who leave the free trial because they didn't have enough timewithout significantly reducing the number of students to continue past the free trial and eventually complete the course. Therefore, if the gross-conversion decreases but the retention and the net-conversion does not fall below a statistically significant level(also need to consider the practical significance level for the business), this can be considered a successful experiment and we can go ahead and launch the change.

 

To summarize, below are the invariant and evaluation metrics chosen for this experiment:

 

Invariant Metrics:

         Number of cookies: number of cookies to view the course overview page

         Number of clicks: number of unique cookies to click the Start free trial button

         Click-through-probability: number of unique cookies to click the Start free trial button divided by the number of unique cookies to view the course overview page

Evaluation Metrics:

         Gross conversion: number of user-ids to complete checkout and enroll in the free trial divided by the number of unique cookies to click the Start free trial button.

         Retention: number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14-day boundary (and thus, make at least one payment) divided by the number of user-ids to complete checkout.

         Net conversion: number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14-day boundary (and make at least one payment) divided by the number of unique cookies to click the Start free trial button.

Summary

 

The results of the sign test are in line with the effect-size test that the change in gross conversion rate is significant but the change in net conversion rate is not. Although, our requirement was that the drop in net conversion rate should be not significant, we need to consider the practical significance boundary i.e. the business viability carefully.

 

Initially, we selected three metrics from which we needed to drop retention rate due to the large number of days that would have been needed in order to run the experiment. So, we came down to the metrics gross-conversion rate and net-conversion rate, both of which need to match the criteria in order for us to launch the experiment. Now, since we have multiple metrics, all of which need to match the expectations, the risk if at least one of them not being able to reject the null just due to random chance, when in reality the results are as per expectations, increases. In other words, the risk of making a type 2 error, β increases. The Bonferroni correction helps us in reducing the value of αoverall by setting all the αindividual. It does not help us reduce β.

Hence, we do not use the Bonferroni correction.

 

Recommendation

For our experiment to be successful, we needed the gross-conversion rate to decrease and the net-conversion not to decrease significantly. The gross conversion rate dropped by around 2 percent and therefore supports our hypothesis. However, the confidence interval for the net-conversion rate is [-0.0116, 0.0019]. The statistical significance boundary for this metric is 0 and the practical significance boundary is -.0075. The confidence interval includes both the statistical significance boundary as well as the practically significant boundary. In other words, the left extreme of the confidence interval lies below the statistical significance and practical significance boundaries i.e. the net-conversion rate may fall below to a point which may matter to business. As mentioned in the Summary section above, we needed both our metrics to match the criteria, which we fail to do in this case. Hence, we should not proceed with the launch.

 

Follow-Up Experiment

From the current experiment, it seems that on providing the students with the before-hand warning, people who thought they would be able to spend less than 5 hours weekly didnt join the 14 day trial. But this message would also be seen by the students who would normally spend more than 5 hours. A better approach would be to track the number of hours spent by a student during the 14 day trial and at the end of the first week, the students who spent less than 5 hours get the pop up message indicating that the course requires greater commitment on their behalf. The message can also include links to motivational videos of Udacity alumni and their success stories.

 

The hypothesis is that hopefully, this would motivate the students to spend more time. The control group would not get any such pop up message. Moreover, the students who spend more than 5 hours would not see this message in either group and hence the change wont impact them. The motivational videos would be available to everyone under resources section.

 

The evaluation metric for this experiment can be retention rate as this gives a true measure of how many students Udacity is able to retain. We can use user-id as the unit of diversion to divert the students into control and experiment groups. The invariant metric would be number of user-ids. This experiment would take a longer time to run, as we saw that testing the retention rate requires a large number of page views.
